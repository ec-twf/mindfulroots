5-HTP (5-hydroxytryptophan) is a compound your body makes from tryptophan and converts into serotonin. 5-HT (5-hydroxytryptamine) is the formal chemical name for serotonin itself. So if you are comparing them as two products to choose between, the short answer is that you cannot: they are two consecutive steps in the same pathway, not rivals. 5-HTP is what you can take. 5-HT is what your body makes from it. One is a supplement, the other is a neurotransmitter, and only the precursor is something you buy.

If you are looking at 5-HTP for mood or sleep, our [5-HTP hub](/products/5-htp/) covers the evidence and the safety catch. This post clears up the naming confusion so you can read labels without being misled.

## The verdict, before the biochemistry

Nobody actually stands at a shelf choosing between a 5-HTP bottle and a 5-HT bottle, because a useful 5-HT (serotonin) supplement does not exist. Serotonin taken by mouth cannot get into the brain. The blood-brain barrier blocks it. So the only practical way to nudge brain serotonin with a precursor is 5-HTP, which crosses that barrier and is then converted into serotonin inside the brain.

When you see 5-HT mentioned on a supplement page, it is describing the destination of the pathway, not an ingredient you can swallow. Keep that straight and most of the confusion disappears.

## How the pathway works

Serotonin is built in a short chain. The amino acid tryptophan is turned into 5-HTP by an enzyme, and 5-HTP is then turned into 5-HT, which is serotonin. Each step is a chemical conversion, and each product in the chain has different properties.

The property that matters most is brain access. As [Turner and colleagues (2006)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pharmthera.2005.06.004) explain in their review of 5-HTP, the precursor crosses the blood-brain barrier freely and without needing a transporter molecule, which is why oral 5-HTP can influence brain serotonin at all. Serotonin itself lacks that easy access, so raising it means supplying an earlier building block, not the finished chemical.

## A head-to-head, for the record

Even though they are not competitors, laying the two side by side makes the distinction concrete.

| Feature | 5-HTP | 5-HT (serotonin) |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | Serotonin precursor | The neurotransmitter itself |
| Position in pathway | One step before serotonin | The end product |
| Crosses blood-brain barrier | Yes | No (taken orally) |
| Sold as a supplement | Yes | Not in a brain-useful form |
| Typical role | Raise serotonin availability | Signal in the nervous system |

The row that decides everything is brain access. Because oral serotonin cannot reach the brain and 5-HTP can, the precursor is the only member of this pair that works as a supplement.

## What the mood evidence says

The naming clarity does not settle whether 5-HTP helps mood, so it is worth being honest about the tier of evidence. A Cochrane review by [Shaw, Turner, and Del Mar (2002)](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD003198) found that 5-HTP and tryptophan performed better than placebo for depression in the available trials, but flagged that the studies were small and of uneven quality, so the finding was cautious rather than a green light. A broad 2020 review by [Maffei (2020)](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms22010181) covered the biochemistry, uses, and toxicology of 5-HTP and reached a similar place: biologically plausible, some supportive human data, and a clear need for better trials. That puts 5-HTP at an emerging evidence tier for mood, not a well-established one.

## Typical use

Where people use 5-HTP, common practice is 50 to 100 mg, sometimes with vitamin B6 as a cofactor, because B6 is needed for the conversion step. Because of the interaction cautions below, 5-HTP is best used only under the guidance of a doctor or pharmacist rather than self-prescribed, especially if you take any prescription medication. There is no equivalent dosing conversation for oral 5-HT, because it is not a supplement that reaches the brain.

## Cautions and interactions

Do not combine 5-HTP with SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, triptans, tramadol, stimulants like Adderall, or other serotonergic drugs without medical supervision, because the combination risks serotonin syndrome, a rare but potentially dangerous reaction described by [Boyer and Shannon (2005)](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMra041867). Avoid 5-HTP in pregnancy. Because 5-HTP acts directly on the serotonin pathway, it is the one supplement we most strongly recommend discussing with a clinician before trying.
