**Verdict first:** magnesium glycinate and magnesium bisglycinate are the *same compound*: magnesium bound to two molecules of the amino acid glycine. "Bisglycinate" is simply the more precise chemical name; "glycinate" is the shortened label term the supplement industry adopted for shelves. If you are trying to choose between a bottle marked "glycinate" and one marked "bisglycinate," you are not choosing between two ingredients at all. What actually matters is the elemental magnesium dose, whether the chelate is fully reacted, and third-party testing. Those are covered below, with the criteria collected on our [magnesium glycinate](/products/magnesium-glycinate/) page.

## Why the two names exist

A chelate is a mineral bonded to an organic carrier. In this case one magnesium ion (Mg²⁺, which carries two positive charges) binds two glycine molecules to balance that charge, so the chemically complete name is magnesium **bis**glycinate, "bis" meaning two. Decades ago, consumer labels shortened this to magnesium glycinate, and the short form stuck. Independent references like the [Examine magnesium entry](https://examine.com/supplements/magnesium/) and the [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements magnesium fact sheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Magnesium-HealthProfessional/) treat them as one and the same form. Any blog that presents "glycinate vs bisglycinate" as a genuine head-to-head between two absorption profiles is comparing a word with its own abbreviation.

## What actually differs between products

Since the molecule is identical, the meaningful variation lives elsewhere:

| Factor | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| **Elemental magnesium per serving** | The number you actually dose by, not the total chelate weight, which is mostly glycine |
| **Fully reacted vs "buffered"** | Some cheaper blends mix in magnesium oxide and still call it glycinate; oxide is worse tolerated and less bioavailable |
| **Third-party testing** | Confirms identity, potency, and low contaminants |
| **Serving size** | Some products need 3-4 capsules to reach a meaningful dose |

A "buffered bisglycinate" that quietly contains magnesium oxide can behave quite differently from a fully reacted glycinate, but that difference is about the *oxide filler*, not the glycinate-versus-bisglycinate wording.

## The evidence, honestly

Magnesium is an essential mineral involved in hundreds of enzymatic reactions, including those governing the stress response and sleep regulation. The glycinate/bisglycinate form is popular for stress and sleep support because it is gentle on the gut and does not have the laxative pull of magnesium citrate or oxide. The [NIH ODS fact sheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Magnesium-HealthProfessional/) rates overall magnesium status as important for health, while noting that supplement trials for mood and anxiety are still emerging rather than definitive. A review of organic magnesium salts found chelated forms generally well absorbed and tolerated ([Schuchardt & Hahn, 2017](https://doi.org/10.2174/1573401313666170427162740)). Treat magnesium as *support* for stress and sleep, not a treatment for an anxiety disorder.

## What to look for in a magnesium bisglycinate supplement for anxiety

Ignore the glycinate-vs-bisglycinate wording and judge the product on:

- **Elemental magnesium per serving**, commonly 100-200 mg per dose; read the "elemental" line, not the chelate weight.
- **Fully reacted chelate**, ideally with no added magnesium oxide (watch for "buffered" as a hint that oxide is blended in).
- **Third-party testing** for identity and purity.
- **A realistic serving size** you can take consistently in the evening.

## Cautions

Magnesium supplements can cause loose stools at higher doses (less so with the glycinate form), and people with reduced kidney function should not supplement magnesium without medical supervision, since impaired clearance can let levels build up. Magnesium can also interact with certain antibiotics and other medicines. Talk with your prescriber before starting if you take medication or have a kidney condition.
