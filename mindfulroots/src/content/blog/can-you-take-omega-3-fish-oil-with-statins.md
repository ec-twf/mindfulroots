Omega-3 fish oil is a concentrated source of the long-chain fatty acids EPA and DHA, taken to support cardiovascular and mood health. If you have been prescribed a statin such as atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, or simvastatin, it is reasonable to ask whether adding fish oil is safe. The short answer is that the two are generally considered compatible, and in some cases a prescription omega-3 is deliberately paired with a statin. Still, this is a heart-medication question, so it deserves a careful, evidence-based look before you combine them. When in doubt, route the decision through the clinician who manages your [omega-3 fish oil](/products/omega-3-fish-oil/) use and your statin together.

## Why the pairing comes up so often

Statins and omega-3s address different parts of a blood-lipid panel. Statins mainly lower LDL ("bad") cholesterol by blocking an enzyme in the liver's cholesterol pathway. Omega-3s mainly lower triglycerides, a separate fat fraction. Because many people with high LDL also have high triglycerides, cardiologists frequently use both. The most-cited example is the REDUCE-IT trial, in which a high-dose prescription EPA product taken *alongside* statin therapy reduced cardiovascular events compared with statin plus placebo ([Bhatt et al., 2019](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1812792)). That trial is one reason the combination is viewed as complementary rather than conflicting.

## The theoretical risks worth knowing

Two mechanisms explain most of the caution around this pairing.

**Bleeding.** High doses of EPA and DHA modestly reduce platelet aggregation, which can lengthen bleeding time. On its own this is usually minor, but it matters if you also take an anticoagulant or antiplatelet drug, or if you are approaching surgery. The [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Omega3FattyAcids-HealthProfessional/) notes that clinically significant bleeding from omega-3 alone is uncommon at typical supplement doses, but the risk rises when other blood-thinning agents are stacked on top.

**Liver enzymes.** Statins are metabolised in the liver and can, in a minority of people, raise transaminases. Omega-3s can occasionally do the same at high doses. The overlap is usually managed by the routine liver monitoring you may already have on a statin, rather than by avoiding fish oil.

Notably, fish oil is *not* a strong inhibitor of the CYP450 enzymes that drive most dangerous statin drug interactions (grapefruit juice and certain antibiotics are the classic offenders there), so a direct pharmacokinetic clash is not the main concern.

## What the evidence summary looks like

Across trials that combined omega-3s with statin therapy, the fatty acids did not appear to reduce the statin's cholesterol-lowering benefit, and the safety profile of the combination was broadly similar to the statin alone. Independent monographs from the [NIH ODS](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Omega3FattyAcids-HealthProfessional/) and [Memorial Sloan Kettering](https://www.mskcc.org/cancer-care/integrative-medicine/herbs/omega-3-fatty-acids) treat moderate-dose fish oil as low-risk for most adults, with the bleeding caveat above. This is supportive evidence for compatibility, not a guarantee for every individual, and it does not replace personalised advice. ## What to look for in an omega-3 fish oil supplement for statin users

If your prescriber is comfortable with you adding fish oil, the product itself still matters. Useful selection criteria:

- **Stated EPA + DHA per serving.** Read the combined active dose, not the "1000 mg fish oil" front-label number. Match the dose to what your clinician suggests rather than guessing.
- **Third-party testing.** Independent verification (for purity and potency) is the single most useful signal on a shelf full of similar bottles.
- **Low oxidation.** A low TOTOX or freshness guarantee means the oil has not gone rancid, which affects both tolerability and quality.
- **A form you tolerate.** Triglyceride and ethyl-ester forms both work; choose one that does not cause reflux for you.

The supplement's role is supportive; it is not a substitute for prescribed therapy.

## Cautions and the bottom line

Pause supplemental fish oil before elective surgery if your team advises it, be extra cautious if you also take warfarin or an antiplatelet, and report any unexplained bruising, bleeding, or liver-related symptoms. Fish oil is a support, not a treatment, and it does not replace a statin's job.

Because statins are prescription cardiovascular medicines, do not start, stop, or dose fish oil around them based on a web page. **Talk to the prescriber who manages your statin before combining it with omega-3 fish oil.** They can weigh your bleeding risk, liver status, and other medications in one place.
