If your mood reliably dips in the darker months, vitamin D is one of the first things worth checking. It's not a cure for seasonal low mood, but it's one of the most common and easily corrected deficiencies linked to it.

## A vitamin that acts like a hormone

Vitamin D receptors are scattered throughout the brain, including regions involved in mood regulation. Most of our supply normally comes from sunlight on skin — which is exactly why indoor lifestyles and northern winters set the stage for shortfall.

## Who runs low

You're statistically more likely to be deficient if you work indoors, live far from the equator, have darker skin, are older, or cover up for cultural or sun-safety reasons. That's a large slice of the population.

## The smartest approach: test, don't guess

The trial evidence is clearest when supplementation corrects a *measured* deficiency. A simple blood test tells you whether you actually need to top up — and how much.
