Glycine is a non-essential amino acid that also acts as an inhibitory (calming) neurotransmitter in the brain and spinal cord. People take it for sleep quality, and the dosage question has a clear anchor: 3 grams about an hour before bed, which is the amount used in the trials that made glycine popular for sleep. Higher amounts show up in research on other endpoints, but for someone reaching for glycine at night, 3 g is the number to start from.

If you would rather get glycine as part of a mineral your body also uses for calm, [magnesium glycinate](/products/magnesium-glycinate/) delivers glycine bound to magnesium, and our [sleep guide](/guides/sleep/) covers where each option fits. This post is about glycine dosing itself.

## How glycine is thought to work

Glycine does two useful things around bedtime. It acts directly as a calming signal at glycine and NMDA receptors, and it appears to lower core body temperature slightly, which is part of the normal wind-down into sleep.

A [2015 study by Kawai and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1038/npp.2014.326) traced glycine's sleep effect to NMDA receptors in the suprachiasmatic nucleus, the brain's master clock, along with a small drop in core temperature and increased blood flow to the skin. That mechanism lines up with what people report: falling asleep a bit faster and waking feeling less foggy, rather than a heavy sedative knockout.

## What the dosing evidence shows

Most of the human sleep work clusters around the same 3 g bedtime dose.

| Study | Design | Dose | Key finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Yamadera 2007 | Crossover, adults | 3 g before bed | Better subjective sleep, easier waking |
| Inagawa 2006 | Restricted-sleep adults | 3 g before bed | Less daytime fatigue after short sleep |
| Bannai 2012 | Review of glycine work | 3 g | Improved sleep quality and next-day function |

The most cited single trial is [Yamadera and colleagues (2007)](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1479-8425.2007.00262.x), a crossover study in which 3 g of glycine before bed improved subjective sleep quality and left people feeling more refreshed. A companion line of work summarized by [Bannai and Kawai (2012)](https://doi.org/10.1254/jphs.11R04FM) reported better sleep satisfaction and less next-day sleepiness at the same dose. These are small, short studies measuring how people feel rather than large trials with objective sleep-lab endpoints, so read the tier as promising, not established.

The practical takeaway: start at 3 g in the hour before bed. There is little reason to jump higher for sleep, since the benefit in these trials showed up at 3 g and larger doses were not tested for better sleep specifically.

## What to look for in a glycine supplement for sleep

If you have settled on trying glycine, the buying decision is refreshingly simple, because glycine is a single, cheap, well-characterized amino acid.

- **A verified 3 g serving.** That is the studied dose. Check the label delivers it in a reasonable amount of powder or capsules rather than a token amount.
- **Plain glycine, few fillers.** You are buying one ingredient. A short label is a good sign.
- **Third-party testing.** Independent verification confirms identity and purity, which matters more than any brand story.

## Cautions and who should check first

Glycine is well tolerated for most healthy adults at 3 g, with occasional mild stomach upset the main complaint. A few groups should not freelance it.

If you have kidney or liver disease, your body's handling of amino acids may differ, so clear glycine with your clinician. If you take clozapine, note that glycine has been studied as something that can reduce that drug's effect, so it is a real conversation for your prescriber rather than a casual add-on. Pregnancy and breastfeeding sit outside the tested range, and glycine is not a fix for a diagnosed sleep disorder like sleep apnea or chronic insomnia, which deserve a proper assessment.

If poor sleep is tangled up with low mood or anxiety, our [get help](/get-help/) page points to real support, and it is worth naming that to a clinician rather than treating it with an amino acid alone. Anyone on regular medication should treat glycine as a quick check with a pharmacist first.
