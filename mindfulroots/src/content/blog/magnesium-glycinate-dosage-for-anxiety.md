If you're searching for a magnesium glycinate dose for anxiety, you probably already know the general idea that magnesium has a calming reputation, and you just want a number. This page gives you the range the research actually uses, but it leads with an honest frame, because the dose only makes sense alongside it: magnesium is gentle, supportive nutrition, not a treatment for an anxiety disorder. The evidence is modest, and it matters most for people who were low in magnesium to begin with.

With that said, here's what the numbers look like.

## The dosage range in plain terms

Most magnesium supplement regimens sit between **200 and 400 mg of elemental magnesium per day**. Studies that have looked specifically at anxiety and stress have generally used doses toward the upper end of that band, commonly in the region of 250 to 400 mg daily.

In practice, many people don't start at the top. A common approach is to begin at 100–200 mg in the evening, see how it's tolerated over a week or two, and increase gradually if needed. Starting low has a practical benefit: it lets your gut adjust and reduces the chance of the loose stools that higher magnesium doses can cause.

The one figure you can't ignore is **elemental magnesium**. Labels often lead with the weight of the whole compound, which is a much bigger number than the magnesium it actually delivers. Find "elemental magnesium" on the Supplement Facts panel and dose off that.

## What the evidence really shows

It's worth being straight about the state of the research, because it shapes how much to expect.

A number of studies suggest magnesium supplementation may take the edge off mild anxiety and stress symptoms, and the effect appears more likely in people with low magnesium intake or higher baseline stress. But systematic reviews have repeatedly flagged that the trials are small, varied in design, and mixed in quality. A 2017 systematic review in *Nutrients* concluded there was suggestive evidence for a benefit on subjective anxiety but that better-designed trials were needed. More recent reviews looking at anxiety and sleep together have reached similarly cautious conclusions.

The honest reading: there's a real signal, it's modest, and it's not a substitute for treatment where treatment is warranted.

## Why glycinate specifically

The trial evidence isn't strongly tied to one form of magnesium, so glycinate isn't chosen for anxiety because studies prove it's uniquely effective. It's chosen because it makes *daily* use comfortable. Glycinate is well absorbed and gentle on digestion, so it avoids the laxative effect that citrate can have, which is exactly the problem you'd hit taking magnesium every day for weeks. The glycine it's paired with also has its own calming associations, which is part of why it's the form people reach for in the evening.

If you're weighing glycinate against citrate more broadly, we've written a [short comparison](/blog/magnesium-glycinate-vs-citrate/) on choosing between the two.

## Timing and consistency

Two practical points do more than the exact milligram figure:

**Timing.** Evening dosing, one to two hours before bed, suits both the goal (calm, better sleep) and the form. If you're splitting a larger dose, afternoon-plus-evening works.

**Consistency.** Where magnesium has helped in studies, the benefit built over weeks of steady daily use, not from a single dose. Treat it as a slow, cumulative thing. Give it a few weeks before deciding whether it's doing anything, and don't chase a stronger effect by pushing the dose up quickly.

A note some clinicians raise: magnesium is sometimes paired with vitamin B6 in anxiety-focused formulas, and a few studies have looked at the combination. That's worth being aware of when reading labels, but it isn't a reason to go hunting for a specific stack on your own.

## Safety: read this part

The calming reputation shouldn't make magnesium feel consequence-free.

- **Upper limit.** Health authorities set a tolerable upper limit of **350 mg per day for supplemental magnesium** (magnesium from food isn't counted toward this). Commonly used anxiety doses can approach that, so it's not a nutrient to megadose casually.
- **Kidney disease.** If your kidneys don't clear magnesium normally, supplements can build up to dangerous levels. Don't supplement magnesium with kidney disease without medical guidance.
- **Medications.** Magnesium can interfere with the absorption of some medications, including certain antibiotics and bisphosphonates, usually managed by spacing the doses apart. Check with a pharmacist if you take regular medication.
- **This isn't treatment.** If anxiety is interfering with your daily life, please treat that as a reason to talk to a professional rather than to raise the dose. See our [crisis and professional support resources](/get-help/).

Used within these bounds, magnesium glycinate is a low-risk thing to try, and the dosing question has a simple answer: stay in the 200–400 mg elemental range, start at the low end, take it in the evening, and give it a few weeks.
