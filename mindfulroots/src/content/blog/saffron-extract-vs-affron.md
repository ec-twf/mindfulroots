If you're comparing "saffron extract" against "affron," the most useful thing to know up front is that these aren't two competing ingredients. affron® is a saffron extract. It's a specific, branded, standardized extract of the saffron stigma (*Crocus sativus*), produced to a fixed marker-compound level and studied in its own human trials. So the real question isn't which herb to pick. It's whether you want a generic saffron extract or this particular named one, and what the label difference buys you.

Here's the short answer. If you want a form that matches what the research tested, choose a standardized branded extract like affron. If you just want saffron and you trust the brand's sourcing, a generic extract can be fine, but you lose the ability to check it against the evidence. Our [saffron extract product page](/products/saffron-extract/) walks through the specific standardized options and where to find them.

## Why "saffron vs affron" is a bit of a trick question

Saffron is the dried stigma of the crocus flower, and by weight it's one of the most expensive spices on earth. Its mood-relevant compounds, crocin and safranal, are thought to influence how the brain handles serotonin. But raw saffron varies enormously by harvest, storage, and processing, so the amount of active compound in any given "saffron extract" is a moving target unless someone has measured and standardized it.

That's what a branded extract does. affron is standardized by HPLC to contain at least 3.5% lepticrosalides (its measured marker compounds) and is dosed at around 28–30 mg per day, the amount used in its trials. Satiereal® is a different branded saffron extract, standardized to 0.3% safranal and dosed higher at roughly 88 mg. Both are still saffron. They've just been pinned to a known concentration so a study can be repeated and a shopper can verify what they're getting.

## What each label actually tells you

| | Generic "saffron extract" | affron® (branded standardized) |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | Saffron stigma extract | Saffron stigma extract |
| Active content stated | Often not | Yes, ≥3.5% lepticrosalides |
| Studied dose | Unknown | 28–30 mg/day |
| Own trial record | Rarely | Yes, several RCTs |
| Best for | Trusting a known brand | Matching the research |

The table makes the point cleanly: the ingredient is the same. The difference is verification. A generic extract may be excellent, but the label gives you nothing to check. A standardized branded extract tells you the marker level and comes with the studies that used that exact material.

## The evidence sits with the standardized extracts

Most of saffron's mood research has been done on standardized extracts rather than loose powder, which is part of why the branded forms matter. A 2013 meta-analysis of randomized trials found saffron supplementation was more effective than placebo for symptoms of low mood in adults with mild-to-moderate presentations. Later trials using affron specifically reported improvements in mood and stress scores in both adults and adolescents over four to eight weeks.

| Year | Extract | Population | Key finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2013 | Various standardized | Adults, low mood | Better than placebo in meta-analysis |
| 2017 | affron® 28 mg | Healthy adults | Lower stress and mood scores |
| 2018 | affron® 14–28 mg | Youth | Improved mood ratings vs placebo |

This is a Moderate evidence tier, not a well-settled one. The trials are mostly small and short, and saffron is best framed as gentle, evidence-aware support for everyday low mood rather than a treatment for diagnosed depression. If your low mood is persistent or severe, that's a conversation for a clinician, and the [/get-help/](/get-help/) page lists places to start.

## So which should you buy?

Choose a standardized branded extract if you want your supplement to match the studied product. affron at 28–30 mg is the most-published option, so it's the closest thing to "what the research used." Satiereal is a valid alternative with its own trial record, just dosed higher because it's less concentrated per milligram.

Choose a generic saffron extract only if you trust the brand's sourcing and testing, since you're relying on that reputation instead of a stated marker level. Given how costly real saffron is, a suspiciously cheap "saffron extract" is a reason to be skeptical rather than a bargain.

Either way, this is one ingredient, not two. The decision is about standardization and evidence-matching, not about picking the right plant.

## Typical use

Saffron trials commonly use 28–30 mg per day of a standardized extract, often split into a morning and evening dose, run consistently for four to eight weeks before judging any effect. Saffron isn't a fast-acting calmative; like most mood botanicals, it's assessed over weeks, not hours.

## Cautions and interactions

Saffron is generally well tolerated at these supplement doses. High doses should be avoided in pregnancy. Because it has been studied for mood, talk to your doctor before combining it with a prescribed antidepressant or other serotonergic medication. If you have a bleeding disorder or take blood thinners, raise it with a pharmacist first.
