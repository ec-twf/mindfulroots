If you've narrowed your magnesium choice down to glycinate and citrate, the good news is you've already eliminated the weak option. Both are well-absorbed forms, both are a real step up from cheap magnesium oxide, and either one will do the basic job of topping up your magnesium. So this isn't a case of one being right and the other wrong. It's a case of matching the form to your gut and your goal.

Here's the short version before the detail: choose **glycinate** if your aim is calm, sleep or daily use without digestive drama. Choose **citrate** if you also want help with occasional constipation, or if cost is the deciding factor.

*(For the full three-way picture including magnesium oxide, and the evidence behind magnesium for sleep and stress, see our [magnesium forms guide](/blog/magnesium-stress-sleep-guide/). This post zooms in on the two forms most people are actually deciding between.)*

## The thing they have in common

Both glycinate and citrate are absorbed reasonably well. Glycinate is magnesium bound to the amino acid glycine; citrate is magnesium bound to citric acid. In both cases the pairing helps the mineral survive digestion and get across the gut wall more effectively than magnesium oxide, which is poorly absorbed (a commonly cited figure is around 4%).

So for the simple purpose of raising your magnesium intake, neither is a bad choice. The differences that matter are downstream of absorption: what each one does to your digestion, and what secondary job it happens to do well.

## Where they diverge: your gut

This is the single most useful distinction, so it's worth being clear about.

**Citrate has a mild laxative effect.** It draws water into the intestines, which softens stool and encourages a bowel movement. That's not a side effect so much as a documented use: magnesium citrate is sold specifically for occasional constipation. At supplement doses it's gentle, but if you're prone to loose stools, or you're taking a larger dose, you'll notice it.

**Glycinate is gentle.** It's far less likely to loosen stools at the same elemental dose, which is exactly why it's the default recommendation for people taking magnesium daily, or taking it in the evening when a midnight trip to the bathroom would defeat the purpose.

If you take nothing else from this comparison, take that: citrate moves things along, glycinate doesn't.

## For sleep and stress: glycinate

For evening wind-down, stress resilience and sleep quality, glycinate is the more comfortable choice for most people. Part of that is the digestion point above, and part is the glycine itself, which has its own calming reputation and is part of why the glycinate form feels well suited to nighttime use.

The evidence for magnesium and sleep is real but modest, and it isn't specific to one form (our [main guide](/blog/magnesium-stress-sleep-guide/) walks through the trials). The practical case for glycinate here isn't that it's dramatically more effective. It's that it delivers well-absorbed magnesium without the gut effect you don't want at bedtime.

## For regularity: citrate

Flip the goal and citrate wins. If you deal with occasional constipation and you also want to top up magnesium, citrate does both at once, and it usually costs less per serving. There's no sense paying more for the gentler form if the thing you're trying to avoid is the thing you actually want.

## Reading the label: elemental magnesium

One trap catches people comparing these two: the number on the front of the bottle is often the weight of the whole compound, not the magnesium inside it. A capsule advertising "1,000 mg of magnesium citrate" contains far less than 1,000 mg of actual magnesium, because most of that weight is the citric acid.

Ignore the headline number. Find **elemental magnesium** on the Supplement Facts panel and compare that. It's the only figure that lets you line up two products fairly, and it's how you'd check that a glycinate and a citrate are actually delivering comparable doses.

## A quick decision guide

- **Sleep, stress, daily use, sensitive stomach** → glycinate
- **Occasional constipation plus magnesium** → citrate
- **Tight budget, no digestive concerns** → citrate is cheaper
- **Not sure** → glycinate is the safer default, because the failure mode (loose stools) is the thing most people are trying to avoid

Whichever you choose, check the elemental magnesium figure, keep the dose in the commonly used 200–400 mg elemental range unless a clinician has said otherwise, and don't supplement magnesium at all without medical guidance if you have kidney disease.
