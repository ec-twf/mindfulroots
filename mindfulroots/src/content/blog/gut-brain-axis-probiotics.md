The notion that gut bacteria could nudge your mood sounds like a stretch — right up until you look at how much signalling runs between the two.

## A two-way conversation

The gut and brain are linked by the vagus nerve and a constant exchange of chemical messengers. A large share of the body's serotonin is actually produced in the gut, and the bacteria living there are part of that chemistry.

## "Psychobiotics"

Researchers now use the term *psychobiotics* for specific bacterial strains shown to influence stress and mood markers in trials — for example certain Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains. The key word is *specific*: it's the named strain that was studied, not "probiotics" in general or a big CFU number.

## How early is this, really?

Early. Promising, well-tolerated, and biologically plausible — but early. That's why we rate this area as *emerging* rather than established. It's a reasonable, low-risk thing to explore, ideally as part of a broader "look after your gut" approach rather than a targeted mood fix.
