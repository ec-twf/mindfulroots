Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is the form of vitamin D your skin makes from sunlight, and vitamin K2 (menaquinone) is a fat-soluble vitamin that switches on the proteins your body uses to place calcium in bone. Can you take them together? Yes, and there is a good reason the two so often share a softgel. This is a pairing built on how they work, not a combination to be nervous about. The single caveat that carries real weight is anticoagulation, which the last section covers in full.

If you are choosing a D3 product and weighing whether the K2 version is worth it, our [vitamin D3 hub](/products/vitamin-d3/) lays out forms, doses, and how to pick. This post stays on the D3-and-K2 question.

## Why the two are paired

Vitamin D and vitamin K do different jobs on the same mineral, which is the whole logic of the combination.

D3 is the one people take for mood, immunity, and bone. Once it is converted to its active form, one of its main effects is to increase how much calcium you absorb from food. That is useful, but absorbed calcium still has to end up in the right place.

That is where K2 comes in. It activates two calcium-handling proteins, osteocalcin and matrix Gla-protein. Osteocalcin helps bind calcium into the bone matrix; matrix Gla-protein helps keep calcium out of arteries and soft tissue. Without enough K2, some of those proteins stay inactive. The theory behind pairing the vitamins, laid out in a [2017 review by van Ballegooijen and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1155/2017/7454376), is that raising calcium absorption with D3 while leaving K2 low may not be the most sensible way to supplement. The human trial evidence that this pairing changes hard outcomes is still limited, so treat it as a reasonable rationale rather than a settled fact.

## MK-4 versus MK-7

If you read labels you will see K2 listed as either MK-4 or MK-7. Both are genuine forms of K2, but they behave differently in the body.

| Form | Half-life | Typical dosing | Where it shows up |
|---|---|---|---|
| MK-4 | Short (about an hour) | Several times daily in studies | Some combination products |
| MK-7 | Long (roughly three days) | Once daily, 90–120 mcg | Most D3+K2 softgels |

MK-7's long half-life is the practical reason most once-a-day D3+K2 products use it: a single dose keeps blood levels active through the day. A [2017 review by Schwalfenberg](https://doi.org/10.1155/2017/6254836) summarizes the forms and their handling. The [NIH Office of Dietary Supplements vitamin K fact sheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminK-HealthProfessional/) is the plainest reference if you want the baseline numbers.

## What to look for in a vitamin D3 and K2 supplement

The buy-intent question here is narrow: if you have decided on a combined product, which one is worth your money? Judge it on four specifics rather than the marketing.

- **D3, not D2.** Cholecalciferol (D3) raises blood 25(OH)D more effectively per unit than D2. The [NIH ODS vitamin D fact sheet](https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/) covers this.
- **A sensible D3 dose.** Most maintenance formulas sit between 1,000 and 5,000 IU. Testing your blood level first is the honest way to know where in that range you belong.
- **K2 as MK-7, around 90 to 120 mcg.** This is the form and dose most combination products settle on.
- **An oil or softgel base, plus third-party testing.** Both vitamins are fat-soluble, so a fat carrier helps absorption, and independent testing confirms the label.

Some observational and trial work, including a [2008 study by Jorde and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1365-2796.2008.02008.x), has linked correcting low vitamin D with modest mood changes, though the effect is clearest when you are actually deficient.

## The one interaction that matters

For most people D3+K2 is low-drama. There is one exception that is not optional to know about.

Vitamin K, including K2, is the direct counterweight to warfarin and other vitamin K antagonists. Those drugs work by lowering vitamin K activity to reduce clotting, and your dose is calibrated to your usual vitamin K intake. Adding a K2 supplement, or stopping one, can move your INR and change how well the drug protects you. A [2016 analysis by Violi and colleagues](https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000002895) reviews how dietary and supplemental vitamin K interacts with these anticoagulants. This is not a reason to fear K2. It is a reason to loop in the clinician who manages your warfarin before you start, so any dose adjustment is planned rather than accidental.

A few other groups should check first too: anyone on other anticoagulants or antiplatelet drugs, people with kidney disease affecting calcium handling, and anyone pregnant or nursing. And because D3 is fat-soluble and stored, stay within label guidance on the D3 dose unless a doctor has told you otherwise.
