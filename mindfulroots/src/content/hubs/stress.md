A stress supplement is a nutrient or plant extract taken to support the body's normal response to everyday pressure — not to treat anxiety, burnout, or any diagnosed condition. That distinction matters, because the honest evidence for this category is *moderate*: there are real human trials behind the four supplements below, but the effects are best understood as taking the edge off a normal stress response, not switching stress off. Here is what each one actually supports, drawn from the research, so you can match a choice to how your own stress shows up.

If you only remember one thing: none of these is a substitute for sleep, movement, and — when stress is persistent — a conversation with a professional. See our note on [when to get help](/get-help/).

## Ashwagandha — the most direct evidence for cortisol

Ashwagandha is the supplement with the most direct human data on the stress-hormone pathway. In several randomized trials using standardized KSM-66 root extract, chronically stressed adults taking 300–600 mg daily showed meaningful reductions in serum cortisol versus placebo over about 6–8 weeks — the best-known trial reported roughly a 27.9% drop. The evidence tier here is **Moderate**: the trials are consistent in direction but often small and industry-funded, so read them as encouraging rather than conclusive. This is a daily, build-over-weeks supplement, not a quick fix. See the full breakdown in our [ashwagandha and cortisol evidence review](/blog/ashwagandha-cortisol-research/), and compare the [ashwagandha product options](/products/ashwagandha/).

## Rhodiola — better studied for stress-related fatigue

Rhodiola rosea is an adaptogen studied more for the *fatigue* side of stress than for calm. Where ashwagandha is the one to reach for if your stress reads as wired and cortisol-driven, rhodiola tends to fit stress that leaves you drained and mentally foggy. Its evidence tier is also **Moderate**, with trials pointing to reduced stress-related fatigue rather than a clear cortisol effect. Our [ashwagandha vs rhodiola comparison](/blog/adaptogens-ashwagandha-rhodiola/) walks through which adaptogen suits which pattern; the [rhodiola product page](/products/rhodiola-rosea/) has the specifics.

## Magnesium glycinate — for stress that wrecks your sleep

If your stress mostly shows up at night, magnesium is the sensible place to start. Chronic stress can deplete magnesium, and the glycinate form is well absorbed and gentle on the gut, which makes it a practical choice for winding down. Its evidence for stress and sleep is **Moderate** and mechanism-led rather than dramatic. Our [magnesium for stress and sleep guide](/blog/magnesium-stress-sleep-guide/) covers how glycinate compares with citrate and oxide, and the [magnesium glycinate product page](/products/magnesium-glycinate/) lists what to look for on a label.

## L-theanine — the only one you feel acutely

L-theanine, an amino acid from tea, is the outlier here: it is the one supplement on this list that works *acutely*. It is studied for promoting a calm-but-alert state, often felt within 30–60 minutes, without sedation — useful for a stressful meeting or a racing mind rather than as a slow daily adaptogen. Evidence tier: **Moderate**. Read our [L-theanine and calm focus review](/blog/l-theanine-calm-focus/) for the detail, and the [L-theanine product page](/products/l-theanine/) for forms and dosing.

## How to choose

Match the supplement to the shape of your stress. Wired and cortisol-driven, willing to commit for weeks: **ashwagandha**. Drained and foggy: **rhodiola**. Stress that ruins your sleep: **magnesium glycinate**. Sudden, in-the-moment tension: **L-theanine**. Two other threads worth knowing — B vitamins support the *machinery* of the stress response (B5 helps produce cortisol, B6 supports neurotransmitters), covered in our [B vitamins for stress and adrenal support](/blog/b-vitamins-stress-adrenal/) piece; and omega-3 fish oil appears to *modulate* how the HPA axis responds to stress rather than lowering cortisol outright, as we explain in [fish oil and cortisol](/blog/fish-oil-and-cortisol/).

Add one supplement at a time so you can tell what is actually helping, and talk to a pharmacist or doctor before starting — especially if you take prescription medication. This page is general information, not medical advice; please read our [disclaimer](/disclaimer/).
