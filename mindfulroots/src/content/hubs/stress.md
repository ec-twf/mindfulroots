A stress supplement is a nutrient or plant extract taken to support the body's normal response to everyday pressure. It is not a treatment for anxiety, burnout, or any diagnosed condition. Keep that line in view, because the evidence for this whole category is moderate: there are real human trials behind the four supplements below, but the effect is taking the edge off a normal stress response, not switching stress off. What follows is what each one actually supports, so you can match a choice to how your own stress tends to show up.

One thing worth saying first. None of these replaces sleep, movement, or a conversation with a professional when stress is persistent. Our note on [when to get help](/get-help/) covers that.

## Ashwagandha: the most direct evidence for cortisol

Ashwagandha has the most direct human data on the stress-hormone pathway. In several randomized trials using standardized KSM-66 root extract, chronically stressed adults taking 300 to 600 mg daily showed meaningful reductions in serum cortisol versus placebo over about six to eight weeks. The best-known trial reported roughly a 27.9% drop. The evidence tier is **Moderate**: the trials agree in direction but are often small and industry-funded, so read them as encouraging rather than settled. This one builds over weeks; it is not a quick fix. The full breakdown is in our [ashwagandha and cortisol evidence review](/blog/ashwagandha-cortisol-research/), and the [ashwagandha product options](/products/ashwagandha/) cover what to look for.

## Rhodiola: better studied for stress-related fatigue

Rhodiola rosea is an adaptogen studied more for the fatigue side of stress than for calm. Reach for ashwagandha if your stress reads as wired and cortisol-driven; rhodiola tends to fit the kind of stress that leaves you drained and foggy instead. Its evidence tier is also **Moderate**, with trials pointing to reduced stress-related fatigue rather than a clear cortisol effect. Our [ashwagandha vs rhodiola comparison](/blog/adaptogens-ashwagandha-rhodiola/) walks through which adaptogen suits which pattern, and the [rhodiola product page](/products/rhodiola-rosea/) has the specifics.

## Magnesium glycinate: for stress that wrecks your sleep

If your stress mostly shows up at night, magnesium is the sensible place to start. Chronic stress can deplete it, and the glycinate form is well absorbed and gentle on the gut, which makes it a practical choice for winding down. Its evidence for stress and sleep is **Moderate**, and mechanism-led rather than dramatic. Our [magnesium for stress and sleep guide](/blog/magnesium-stress-sleep-guide/) covers how glycinate compares with citrate and oxide, and the [magnesium glycinate product page](/products/magnesium-glycinate/) lists what to check on a label.

## L-theanine: the only one you feel acutely

L-theanine, an amino acid from tea, is the outlier. It is the one supplement here that works within the hour rather than over weeks. It promotes a calm-but-alert state, usually felt in 30 to 60 minutes, without sedation, which makes it a fit for a stressful meeting or a racing mind rather than a slow daily routine. Evidence tier: **Moderate**. Our [L-theanine and calm focus review](/blog/l-theanine-calm-focus/) has the detail, and the [L-theanine product page](/products/l-theanine/) covers forms and dosing.

## How to choose

Match the supplement to the shape of your stress. Wired and cortisol-driven, willing to commit for weeks: ashwagandha. Drained and foggy: rhodiola. Stress that ruins your sleep: magnesium glycinate. Sudden, in-the-moment tension: L-theanine. Two more threads are worth knowing. B vitamins support the machinery of the stress response, since B5 helps produce cortisol and B6 supports neurotransmitters, which we cover in [B vitamins for stress and adrenal support](/blog/b-vitamins-stress-adrenal/). And omega-3 fish oil seems to change how the HPA axis responds to stress rather than lowering cortisol outright, as we explain in [fish oil and cortisol](/blog/fish-oil-and-cortisol/).

Add one supplement at a time so you can tell what is actually helping, and talk to a pharmacist or doctor before you start, especially if you take prescription medication. This page is general information, not medical advice; please read our [disclaimer](/disclaimer/).
